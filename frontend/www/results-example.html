If you're new to the world of genetics, you may see some terminology with which you're unfamiliar. You might have questions, like what is a PGS? What is a z-score? What is a standard deviation? How does any of this have anything to do with me?


The learning curve of how all of this connects to you doesn't have to be steep. We'll walk you through the basics, and by the end you'll have a starting foundation in what your genetics results mean for you and how they play into the big picture of your life.

üß¨ DNA Fundamentals

For the most part, we've all heard of DNA; but often that's where it ends. We're told it's a blueprint for who we are, how our body knows what to do and how to grow and develop, but how it all works is left as a bit of a mystery. Before we can understand your polygenic score, let's start with the building blocks of genetics itself.


What is DNA?

At some point in your life you probably saw the recognizable shape of the double helix: two twisting parallel lines with climbing rungs between them, like a ladder. Life relies on this macromolecule and the instructions it contains. Nearly all cells have DNA, and with it, they produce the proteins required for the whole biological machine to function.


‚≠ê DNA stands for deoxyribonucleic acid

The Building Blocks: Nucleotides & Base Pairs

DNA is actually beautifully simple, in a sense. Those twisting sides of the ladder‚Äîthe backbones‚Äîare alternating molecules of phosphate and sugar groups, called deoxyribose. Each phosphate and sugar group has one of four nucleobases attached to it: cytosine (C), guanine (G), adenine (A) or thymine (T). A phosphate, sugar and nucleobase, all together, is called a nucleotide.


Series of nucleotides form a long chain together‚Äîand this chain meets up with a complementary partner chain but running in the opposite direction (We call this antiparallel.). It‚Äôs sort of like a million ships passing in the night‚Äîor perhaps two soccer teams of kids lined up with their hands out to ‚Äúside-five‚Äù congratulate each other after a game. Except in this scenario they‚Äôre holding hands and won‚Äôt let go without a third party breaking everyone up.


Each nucleotide's chemical properties forces them to only link up with a specific other nucleotide. This is where the simple beauty starts to occur (we think). There are two categories: pyramidines and purines. The pyramidines are cytosine (C) and thymine (T), and the purines are guanine (G) and adenine (A). Cytosine always links up with guanine (C-G or G-C) and thymine always links up with adenine (T-A or A-T). Two nucleotides make a base pair (bp)‚Äîand these base pairs are what lead to variations in the genome that create diversity and distinctiveness among populations and individuals.


‚≠ê A genome is all of your genetic code. Every last one of the approximate 3 billion base pairs that make up the human genome.

What Are SNPs (Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms)?

This is the whole reason you‚Äôre here. You‚Äôre interested in what makes you you, and the way that happens is through variations in the theme: single-nucleotide polymorphisms, or SNPs (pronounced snips). Less specifically, single-nucleotide variations, or SNVs (No one knows how to pronounce SNVs, but sneevies has been the scuttlebutt lately.), but we prune down to only the variants that occur in greater than 1% of the population‚Äîand those are the SNPs. We consider any variation with a frequency less than that to be rare, and current methodology don‚Äôt support them.


Sometimes a nucleotide gets swapped out, kicked out, introduced to a new neighbor or even itself, and when that happens we get a variant. If we‚Äôre referring to a single variant at a precise location (or locus), then we call it an allele. So, if a specific nucleotide is expected to be thymine based on a reference (think of it like an average human's genetics), but it is instead found to be cytosine, we would refer to both T and C as the possible alleles for that SNP.


‚≠ê There are millions of SNPs in every person, but most don‚Äôt result in any observable changes!


Genetic Variants: How We All Differ

We‚Äôve identified more than 600 million SNPs in the human genome, and that‚Äôs for the whole, global population. That‚Äôs the reference genome, though, or that "average human" aforementioned. For an individual‚Äôs personal genome, typically it differs at only 4 to 5 million loci (plural of locus), and the vast majority of those are SNPs; though, they also include indels‚Äîor insertion-deletions‚Äîwhich belong to the larger family of structural variations, along with duplications, inversions, translocations and more. These involve longer sequences, now expanded to include greater than 50 base pairs (Alkan et al., 2011).


While there are variants that lead to birth defects, diseases and disorders (like Huntington‚Äôs disease, cystic fibrosis or Down‚Äôs syndrome), many are harmless and contribute to the attributes we see around us and make humans diverse creatures. Height, eye color, youthfulness of skin as we age and, as you‚Äôve discovered, educational attainment are all associated with genetic variants.

üîó From DNA to Observable Traits

Now that you understand the basic structure of DNA and genetic variants, let's connect the dots between those tiny molecular differences and the traits we can actually observe and measure‚Äîlike your educational attainment.


Heritability

We‚Äôve gone long enough without bringing up a term that is often misunderstood and‚Äîas a result‚Äîmisused. That word is heritability.


If you haven‚Äôt heard of heritability before, you might immediately jump to another word: inheritability. These are not the same word, and they mean very different things.


‚ÄúHeritability is a concept that summarizes how much of the variation in a trait is due to variation in genetic factors‚Äù (Wray & Visscher, 2008).


That is to say, it‚Äôs the proportion of a trait‚Äîlike your educational attainment‚Äîobserved within a population that can be explained by changes in the genome alone. It is not the chance the trait might be inherited. This is very important, because it allows for the environment to be a variable. The days of arguing over whether it‚Äôs nature or nurture that makes us all wonderful or terrible people. It‚Äôs both.


Photo by Toa Heftiba on Unsplash

Heritability is a push-pull between genetics and environment. If environment‚Äôs input goes up, genetics‚Äô goes down (you can‚Äôt have more than 100%, right?). The output is you (or a banana). And like any good music in life or the stance on the healthfulness of butter, heritability is dynamic. As we learn more, the ratio can change.


‚≠ê Like Batman, science never sleeps either!


Genotype vs. Phenotype

So, what is a phenotype? We'll do you one better: what is a genotype?


Simply put, a phenotype is an observable trait we can measure, like height or eye color or ring fingers longer than pointers. Whereas a genotype is the genetic material. We can't see and measure it with our bare eyes, but we can with some really cool equipment. It's the genotype which helps create the phenotype.


If you want a mnemonic, think of the phenotype as physical while the genotype is genetic (a bit on the nose, isn't it?). But like with your educational attainment, sometimes the phenotype is more abstract and intangible, but still measurable. And you can eventually touch a diploma, so we'll stand by our mnemonic.


Monogenic vs Polygenic Traits

Sometimes one gene is all that's necessary for a trait to exist or change. Those are called monogenic (mono for one), or Mendelian‚Äînamed after Gregor Mendel, an Austrian monk in the mid 19th century and the "father of modern genetics," who bred and studied pea plants. He studied monogenic traits like flower and seed colors or texture in hybrids and offspring generations. Other monogenic traits we can see in humans include things like attached vs unattached earlobes, wet vs dry earwax or the ability to taste PTC (a bitter compound). More seriously, devastating diseases like Huntington's disease, sickle cell anemia and cystic fibrosis are also monogenic.


Most of our traits, however, are not so simple. They are complex or polygenic (poly for many or more than one): many genes and their variants all add up to create a difference between persons. Skin or eye color, height or your educational attainment are complex traits and the result of many genes working or interfering with one another. Height has over 700 genetic variants identified so far that contribute to it!


‚≠ê Not only can one trait be the result of many genes, but one gene can affect many traits! This is called pleiotropy.

Photo by Olga Drach on Unsplash

Discovering Trait-Associated Variants: GWAS

GWAS (pronounced Gee-Waaah-Ss), an acronym for genome-wide association study, is a method used to find all the variants associated with traits and diseases. It scans the genome of thousands to millions of people and then compares the SNPs between people with different trait values (e.g., tall vs short height, married vs divorced or high vs low educational attainment).


GWAS replaced the old candidate gene studies of the late ‚Äò90s and 2000s, the method to blame for the over-hyped discoveries of causal or significantly associated claims for genetics, like serotonin and its role in major depressive disorder (claims that were spectacularly rebuked in a publication by Border et al. in 2019). We used to just suggest to colleagues over drinks, ‚ÄúGene such-and-such is associated with heavy bloating effects on Thanksgiving,‚Äù and the next day start sorting people out into ‚Äúbloating‚Äù or ‚Äúno bloating‚Äù groups. What genes differ? Those must be the genes.


Subsequently, journalists everywhere declare scientists have solved the mystery of why eating a metric ton of turkey and mashed potatoes causes excessive gastrointestinal pain.


GWAS is hypothesis-free, in that we don't come in with a gene picked out for the study. We scan the whole genome for associations between any SNP and the trait we're interested in.


In contrast to those histrionic-inducing candidate gene studies, which had small sample sizes (i.e., not many people studied), GWAS requires very large sample sizes (hundreds of thousands to millions of people). This is because SNPs‚Äîindividual variants‚Äîtypically have incredibly small effects on complex traits. So small, in fact, that the first GWAS disappointed people because of how tiny the effect sizes were (i.e., how important the genes were found to be in its relationship with a trait). But with the increased power (larger sample sizes) this meant that those tiny effect sizes were more accurate, and now we‚Äôre setting off a lot less false alarms thanks to GWAS taking the torch from candidate gene studies.


Photo by National Cancer Institute on Unsplash

With GWAS, and new, stringent statistical rules for when we claim significant relationships, we‚Äôve started to realize just how many SNPs are involved in each of our traits‚Äîand while they all contribute a tiny fraction of variance (often <0.1%), when you put together hundreds to thousands of SNPs, you end up with explanations for substantial heritability.


Which brings us, finally, to your polygenic score. Keep reading.


‚≠ê It‚Äôs important to know GWAS can identify associations, but it doesn‚Äôt prove causation. SNPs can actually just tag along for the ride with their close SNP neighbors when getting stirred up to produce offspring (formally, "recombination" and "inheritance") with the combined genetics of parents. This is called linkage, and the result is what we call linkage disequilibrium. It‚Äôs why we have to be careful before making any claims about a SNP‚Äôs role in a trait.

üìä Understanding Your Polygenic Score

Now that you understand DNA, variants, and how they influence traits, you're ready to understand what your polygenic score actually means!


What is a Polygenic Score?

Abbreviated as PGS, or sometimes PGI for polygenic index (same thing), this is essentially a summed up value of a bunch of your genetic variants and their combined effects upon your phenotype, or trait. In this case, your educational attainment.


Think of it as a little boost to how much your genetics apply toward the trait of interest. It can be used as a predictor (though as we'll learn, genetics are not destiny). In this case, through some math, we can predict the highest level of education you'll attain.


What is a Z-Score?

Your PGS is actually what in statistics is called a z-score. It's not just a score like a judge gives a gymnast at a competition. Instead, it's a score that represents how different you are from the mean (or average) of the population. In your case, it's positive, which means (ha!) you're above average. If it were negative, it means you're below average in that phenotype.


But what does your score of 3.5 mean in terms of how different you are? Well, a z-score is how many standard deviations away from the population average you are, and a standard deviation is a fancy statistical term for a computed value of a unit away from the average. In this case, one standard deviation is calculated to be worth 3 years. Your PGS z-score is 3.5, but because genetics only explain about 9.5% of educational differences (R¬≤ = 0.0946), your predicted deviation is actually 1.076 standard deviations above average. This means a boost of 3.23 years on top of the average 14 years of education in the United States. Total, you're predicted to spend 17.23 years in school, which would mean your genetics are pointing you toward a Master's degree!


üìä How We Calculate Your Predicted Educational Attainment

Step 1: Find the Correlation
\[r = \sqrt{R^2} = \sqrt{0.0946} = 0.3076\]
The correlation tells us the strength of the relationship between your PGS and actual educational attainment.
Step 2: Calculate Predicted Trait Deviation
\[\text{Predicted } z\text{-score} = r \times \text{PGS } z\text{-score} = 0.3076 \times 3.5 = 1.076\]
Your genetics place you 3.5 SD above average genetically, but because genes only explain ~9.5% of variance, your predicted educational deviation is 1.076 SD.
Step 3: Convert to Years of Education
\[\text{Predicted years} = (\text{predicted } z \times \text{SD}) + \text{mean}\] \[= (1.076 \times 3) + 14 = 3.23 + 14 = 17.23 \text{ years}\]
With the U.S. average of 14 years (SD = 3 years), your predicted educational attainment is approximately 17.23 years‚Äîroughly a Master's degree level.

How Much Do Genes Matter?

While your genetics do suggest you have 17.23 years of education ahead of you, that's neither the complete picture, nor is it a fortune teller guaranteeing (or threatening, depending on your perspective) you're going to do all of those years. There's another statistic involved.


R¬≤ (R squared) is how much of the variance (remember how you're different than the average?) is explained by an independent variable (IV). In this case, the IV is your genetics, and the dependent variable, or DV, (what depends upon and changes due to the IV) is your educational attainment.


And in the case of this specific PGS, the R¬≤ is 0.0946. That means your genetics, based on current research, only contributes an approximate 9.5% to your educational attainment. Of calculated PGS out there, this is considered moderate.


The other 90.5%? That's everything else that isn't your DNA. Genetics are not destiny (more on that later).


üìö What Does This Mean For You?

Educational attainment, in this context, refers to the total years of schooling a person completes‚Äîeverything from kindergarten through potentially graduate school and beyond.


This trait (or phenotype) is influenced by many factors: cognitive abilities, personality characteristics like conscientiousness and curiosity, family environment, socioeconomic opportunities, and of course, genetics too.


The genetic component is highly polygenic, meaning thousands of tiny genetic variants each contribute a minuscule effect that adds up. The study used to calculate your score analyzed over 1.1 million individuals of European ancestry, making it one of the largest genetic studies ever conducted (Lee et al., 2018).

üìä Methodology & Data Source

Sample Size & Composition

A 2018 study by Lee et al. combined the data of ~1.1 million individuals from multiple GWAS and consortia, including the UK Biobank, 23andMe and the Social Science Genetic Association Consortium (SSGAC). Because of how small each SNP‚Äôs effect is on educational attainment, enormous statistical power is required through a massive sample size to detect any effects reliably.


Educational attainment was measured as years of schooling completed, from elementary school through doctoral degrees; but it‚Äôs important to remember: the phenotype captures formal education only. It doesn't measure intelligence, knowledge or life success and nepotism.


Notably, however, the sample is predominantly of European ancestry. While continuous work improves the diversity of our reference genomes, our samples still remain highly Eurocentric. This ancestry bias is an ongoing and known issue in genomics research and limits accuracy when applied to non-European ancestries. Remember linkage disequilibrium? There are chunks of our genome that stick together for different, time-separated ancestries, called haploblocks. The result is if someone with a very different set of haploblocks, perhaps Korean instead of German, gets a predictive score about a trait, then the accuracy of that prediction is reduced, since it was built on mostly European references.

How Your PGS Was Calculated

Your polygenic score is a weighted sum of a whole bunch of your variants across the genome. Here's the process:


  1. GWAS identifies associations: The Lee et al. study identified thousands of SNPs that were statistically associated with educational attainment. Each SNP was assigned an "effect size"‚Äîhow much that particular variant influences years of schooling.

  2. Your genotype is scored: For each SNP included in the PGS, we look at your DNA to see which version (allele) you have. Some alleles are associated with higher educational attainment, some with lower, and some are neutral.

  3. Weighted summation: Each of your alleles gets multiplied by its effect size (the "weight"), and then all of these weighted values are added up. The formula looks like this:
    PGS = (Œ≤‚ÇÅ √ó G‚ÇÅ) + (Œ≤‚ÇÇ √ó G‚ÇÇ) + (Œ≤‚ÇÉ √ó G‚ÇÉ) + ... + (Œ≤‚Çô √ó G‚Çô)
    Where:
    • Œ≤ = Pronounced "beta," it's the effect size for each SNP (from the GWAS)
    • G = Your genotype at that SNP (coded as 0, 1, or 2 copies of the effect allele)
    • n = Total number of SNPs included in the score (essentially it means just keep going and multiplying until you run out of betas and genotypes)

    Don't worry, you won't be tested on this.


  4. Standardization: The final sum is standardized to have a mean of 0 and standard deviation of 1 in the discovery sample. Because of this normalizing, your polygenic score is also a z-score (in your case, 3.5).

The PGS used here (PGS002012) includes thousands of genetic variants, each contributing a tiny amount to your overall score. It's highly polygenic and complex. No single gene determines your educational outcomes‚Äîit's the combined effect of many variants across the genome.

‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions

Does this capture pure genetics or environment too?

The PGS is calculated purely from your DNA and its SNPs. There is no environmental data involved. However, the effect sizes come from observational studies where genes and environments were correlated. People with genetic variants associated with higher education often grew up in education-rich environments (gene-environment correlation or rGE); so the prediction captures both direct genetic effects and some indirect effects through correlated environments in the study population. This is a limitation of observational GWAS: you can't fully disentangle nature from nurture.

What should I do with this information?

Use it to better understand yourself. View it as neither a limitation (if a PGS is negative or predicts harmful effects) nor a prescription. Don't make major life decisions based solely on a PGS. Certainly consider healthier habits should they help prevent a negative trait (like Alzheimer's) and feel free to contribute to your genetic predispositions by enhancing your environment, but focus on your interests, passions and opportunities. Those are more important than a prediction.

Can this predict my child's educational attainment?

Not directly. Children inherit ~50% of variants from each parent, but specific combination is random. Your score won't ensure a similar score with your kids. Plus, as stated previously, environment matters for ~90% of the contribution of the trait. Your parenting and the resources and opportunities provided matter more than inherited genetics.

How accurate is this compared to other traits?

R¬≤ = 9.5% is moderate for behavioral and social traits. Height PGS can reach ~25% variance explained (more genetic). Schizophrenia PGS ~7-10% (similar). Complex behavioral traits like educational attainment are heavily influenced by environment though, so 9.5% is actually pretty good for this type of trait.
